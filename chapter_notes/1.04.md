# Chapter 1.4 — The Yurt

**POV:** Alejandro (third person limited)
**Setting:** Yurt at How The Light Gets In festival, Hay-on-Wye. Night of the festival. Summer 2019.
**Time span:** Evening → late night

**Epigraph:** "By paying a certain kind of attention, you can humanise or dehumanise, cherish or strip of all value. By a kind of alienating, fragmenting and focal attention, you can reduce humanity — or art, sex, humour, or religion — to nothing." — Iain McGilchrist, *The Master and His Emissary*

**Prose status:** Not yet drafted

## What This Chapter Does

1. Alejandro processes the festival day — McGilchrist, Persephoni's effortless connection, his own failure to say it first
2. The evening in the yurt — three teenagers, two beds, moonlight through canvas
3. Kathleen initiates. Alejandro and Kathleen lose their virginity to each other.

5. The moonlight is there — he could see. He chooses not to. The Master is available and he shuts it down.

## Thematic Architecture

### The Emissary Closes Its Eyes
Alejandro has just heard McGilchrist explain that the Emissary replaces reality with its own construction. That night, he does exactly this — closes his eyes to block the seeing (the Master), so the Emissary can project a narrative (Persephoni) over the reality (Kathleen). He enacts the usurpation in the most intimate context imaginable, on the same day he learned to name it.

### The Gaslighting Chain
- George overwrites Rosemary's reality (the castle, the pub)
- Rosemary overwrites Persephoni's experience (tender-headed)
- Now Alejandro overwrites Kathleen's. She's having her first time with the boy she loves. He's somewhere else entirely. Her experience — this is real, this is love, this is what it's supposed to be — is being quietly, invisibly replaced.
- Nobody in this chain is being cruel. That's what makes it devastating.

### The "Kathleen" Song Echo
He sings "Kathleen" looking at the fretboard so he doesn't have to look at her face. Now he's with Kathleen with his eyes closed. Same avoidance, escalated from song to sex. The fretboard was a small lie. The closed eyes are a large one.

### The Drawer Breaks
The drawer that doesn't close anymore — this is the night it stops pretending to close. He can file the thought away during a car ride. He cannot file it away while he's inside his girlfriend imagining her best friend. The drawer is open and everything is on the floor.

### Two Lights in the Yurt — The Color Motif
The yurt has two light sources: the wood stove (orange/red, warm, flickering) and moonlight (blue/silver, cool, steady). The crown at the top of the yurt has a round opening where the support poles meet — covered by a small cone of canvas set about a foot above it (a rain cover), creating a ring of open space between the cone and the crown through which smoke escapes and sky is visible.

**The moon tracks.** Early in the night, the moon hasn't found the ring. Moonlight is only diffuse — filtered through canvas walls, faint, the stove winning. The ring shows dark sky and a thin band of stars. The yurt is warm, orange, mostly the Master's light.

**During the sex, the moon finds the ring.** It tracks across the sky until it aligns with the gap between cone and crown, and suddenly direct moonlight angles in — silver-blue and sharp, falling across the beds and pooling on the fur rug. The Emissary's light arrives at full strength at the exact moment Alejandro closes his eyes and begins projecting Persephoni over Kathleen. The light gets in. He shuts his eyes against it.

**After:** An arc of moon is visible through the ring. Alejandro stares up at it — support poles meeting at a ring of wood around a ring of sky around a sliver of moon. The Emissary's light is steady, direct, unwavering — it's not going anywhere.

This foreshadows the **mural in the holding cell** (Act 3, 3.7): red = the Master, blue = the Emissary. The colors Persephoni will one day see mapped onto the two hemispheres are already here in this yurt — the firelight alive and present and warm (the Master's light, the mode of attention that just *sees*), the moonlight cool and distant and constructing shadows (the Emissary's light, the mode that narrates and projects).

Alejandro closes his eyes against both. He refuses the Master's warmth (he won't see Kathleen as she is, present, real, here) and he refuses the Emissary's clarity (he won't look at what he's doing). He shuts down both hemispheres and retreats into pure projection — a third space that is neither seeing nor narrating, just wanting.

This is the anti-thesis of "how the light gets in." Both lights are getting in. He's closing his eyes against all of it.

## Scene Breakdown

### Scene A — Evening at the festival
After food. The three of them wandering the festival at night. String lights, music, the energy of a summer evening. Alejandro is still buzzing from the talk, still turning over Persephoni's line — "why someone can feel something and be told they're not feeling it." She said what he'd been trying to say. She got there first. She always gets there first. He's processing this — admiration, attraction, the ache of it — while walking beside Kathleen, who is holding his hand and happy.

Persephoni is lighter now. The afternoon weight (the pub, the gaslighting chain) has lifted some. She's doing Sheepey bits again. The festival at night is actually beautiful — she can admit this.

**The bookshop / Muffin.** Hay-on-Wye is famous for its bookshops — they're everywhere, tables spilling onto the street, stacked in doorways, filling old cinemas and castles. And everywhere they go, Persephoni keeps seeing a poster for a children's book by a local author: *Muffin and the Passage of Time.* She doesn't know what it is. She hasn't seen the cover. She just keeps seeing the title — on posters, in shop windows, on a chalkboard outside a café — and it delights her more each time.

She tells Alejandro why: the title juxtaposes something as absurd as a muffin with something as heavy as "the passage of time." It shouldn't work. A baked good and mortality. A pastry confronting the infinite. She finds this hilarious and somehow profound and she can't stop talking about it. She's imagining a muffin — an actual muffin — sitting in a library, contemplating the passage of time. A muffin in an existential crisis. A muffin who has seen too much.

She wants a copy. The first bookshop is sold out — "Oh, Muffin! Sold out this morning, love." The second bookshop is sold out. The third sold their last copy an hour ago and seems genuinely sorry about it. Everyone knows about this book. It's a local author, heavily promoted, the kind of small-town literary event that Hay-on-Wye lives for. Which means every shop has a poster and no shop has a copy. She's now on a quest. Alejandro and Kathleen are being dragged from shop to shop while Persephoni asks increasingly urgent questions about a children's book she hasn't even seen yet.

She finally finds it at a small shop near the edge of town — an old woman behind the counter, the kind of shop that smells like dust and bindings and has a cat asleep on the history section. The woman has one copy left. She was saving it for her niece. Persephoni's face does something — the enthusiasm, the delight, the way she holds the book like it's already hers — and the old woman sells it to her. Not reluctantly. Gladly. Because Persephoni's joy is the kind that makes people want to give her things.

**The reveal.** Alejandro reads the back cover. Muffin is a mule. The Passage is an underground tunnel. It's a children's book about a mule named Muffin who finds a tunnel that travels through time. Everything she'd been imagining — the existential pastry, the baked good confronting infinity — is wrong. It's a mule in a tunnel.

She loves this EVEN MORE. The actual premise is better than anything she invented because it's more absurd, not less — someone named a mule Muffin and then sent him through a time tunnel and played it completely straight. She reads bits aloud on the walk back. She does voices. She's already building Muffin's biography the way she built Sheepey's — Muffin has been to the Renaissance and didn't care for it, Muffin once met a Victorian horse who was insufferable about it.

(Muffin is actually a classic BBC puppet character — Muffin the Mule, 1946 — but neither of them knows this. Alejandro would have looked it up on his phone if he'd thought to. He didn't. He was watching her face.)

Kathleen is dying. Alejandro watches Persephoni do this and thinks: *spontaneous mythopoesis.* Again. She's doing it again. She encounters a title and within minutes it's alive, it has a history, it has feelings — first as the existential muffin she invented, then as the time-traveling mule she discovered. She didn't need the correct version to make the story. She didn't need any version. She just needed the spark. The Master doesn't need the label. It just sees the mule.

This is the last purely joyful thing that happens before the night turns. The three of them in a bookshop, laughing, Persephoni making a mule real just by talking about him. It's good, and it's complicated, and nobody is saying the complicated part.

### Scene B — The yurt at night
Back in the yurt. Settling in. The yurt description redux — but now at night. The wood stove is going (maybe George lit it for them). The lantern. The fur rugs. Moonlight through canvas walls, turning the space silver and honey.

Persephoni and Kathleen in the left bed. Alejandro in the right. The girls doing a last Sheepey bit — quieter now, sleepy. Persephoni's voice getting slower. Then silence. The yurt breathing. Wind through canvas. Someone's guitar somewhere across the field, distant.

Alejandro lies awake. He's thinking about the talk. About the Master and the Emissary. About the king in the tower. He's turning over the image — still working on it, still trying to find the version that lands. He's aware of the moonlight, aware of the two shapes in the bed across from him. Persephoni's curls on the pillow. Kathleen's smaller shape curled against her.

### Scene C — Kathleen comes to him
She crosses the gap between the beds. Quietly. Her feet on the fur rug. She slips in beside him — small, warm, deliberate. She's been thinking about this. The car earlier (her hand finding him) was the overture. This is the thing itself.

She doesn't say much. She doesn't need to. Her body is doing the talking the way it did in the car — "a conversation his brain couldn't keep up with." She's brave. She's certain. She wants this. She loves him and she wants to show him and she's sixteen and this is the biggest thing she's ever done.

Key detail: Kathleen initiates with confidence. She's not fumbling or uncertain. She decided this was happening, probably decided it hours ago, maybe days ago. The hand in the car was her telling herself she was ready. Now she's here.

**Positioning:** Alejandro is on his back the entire time. Kathleen is on top. He barely moves — his hands find her hips and stay there, holding the curve of her waist, and he is genuinely attracted to this. She has a shapely bottom, the feature he's always focused on when trying to find the physical pull (established in 1.2). The curve of her waist under his hands is real. He's not faking that. But it's a choice — a thing he can find attractive if he concentrates — and it's nothing compared to the involuntary pull of Persephoni. The position matters: he's passive, receiving, eyes closed. She's the one moving, the one doing, the one who decided. He lets it happen. That's not the same as wanting it.

### Scene D — The sex / eyes closed
Alejandro's body responds before his brain can intervene. This is established — the car scene was the rehearsal for exactly this. "A sudden urgent syllable in a language he hadn't known he was fluent in."

But his brain doesn't stop. His brain never stops. He's classifying even now — heart rate, neurochemistry, the phenomenology of touch. The taxonomic voice is running but it's losing. The body is winning. And in the gap between the body winning and the brain losing, something else fills in: Persephoni.

**The closed eyes.** He closes them when it starts. He has to. The moonlight is coming through the canvas — soft, silver, more than enough to see by — and if he opens them he'll see Kathleen's face. Round. Sweet. Those wide brown eyes looking up at him. And he can't. Not because she isn't beautiful — she is, in her way — but because the Emissary has already started its projection, and if the Master sees what's actually there, the whole thing falls apart.

So he keeps them closed. And behind his eyelids: chocolate curls. Pale green eyes. Golden brown skin. The girl in the other bed. The girl who might be awake, who might be hearing this, who is six feet away on the other side of a low wooden table with a lantern on it.

**Alejandro's brain during:** It oscillates. Moments of pure body — sensation overriding everything, the language he didn't know he spoke — and moments of horrifying clarity where his taxonomic mind offers up observations he doesn't want: *this is your first time and you are imagining someone else. Kathleen's breathing is different from the breathing you're imagining. Her body is smaller than the body you're imagining. You are the Emissary. You are doing the thing. You are doing it right now.*

He shoves each observation into the drawer. The drawer is broken. He shoves anyway.

**The two colors.** He does open his eyes — not for long, not on purpose, but the body moves and the eyes open and for a moment he sees. The geometry of the yurt puts them between the two light sources: the wood stove on one side, the moon through the canvas on the other. Kathleen is bathed in blue — the moonlight catching her face, her skin, her round eyes looking up at him. Cool, silver, still. And his own body is in the orange glow of the stove — warm, flickering, alive. He notices the difference. He catalogs it because he catalogs everything: she is blue and he is orange and the two colors meet somewhere between them. He doesn't know what he's seeing. He doesn't understand it. He closes his eyes again.

But the image stays. And the thing he doesn't think about — can't think about, because it's not his POV to have — is what Kathleen sees when she looks up. From her angle: Alejandro in the orange glow of the stove. Warm. Flickering. Alive. She sees him in the Master's light — sees him with total attention, without narrative, without projection. She is the only person in this yurt who is actually *looking.* And she is looking at a boy lit in red-orange warmth, and what she sees is the boy she loves, and she is right about everything except the one thing that matters.

The key: the colors are about how each person is *being seen*, not who they are. Alejandro sees Kathleen in blue (the Emissary's color — cool, distant, the thing he's narrating over). Kathleen sees Alejandro in red/orange (the Master's color — warm, present, the thing she's fully attending to). Same moment. Two completely different acts of seeing.

**Kathleen during:** We're in Alejandro's POV, so we only get what he can perceive with his eyes closed. Her breathing. Her hands. The sounds she makes. The way she holds him — tightly, like she's afraid he'll leave, or like she wants to press herself into him until there's no space left. She whispers his name once. His name. Not anyone else's. She's here. She's completely here.

**Persephoni during:** Silence from the other bed. He doesn't know if she's asleep. He can't check without opening his eyes, and he can't open his eyes. And he's conflicted about which he wants — conflicted in a way that disgusts him even as he feels it.

Part of him wants her awake. Wants her hearing this. The idea that she might be lying six feet away, listening to him — to the sounds, to Kathleen's breathing, to the bed — does something to him that he can classify precisely and wishes he couldn't. Her participating, even just by observing, even just by being present in the dark, turns him on in a way that shames him. As if her attention, even involuntary, even unwanted, makes the whole thing more real. As if nothing fully exists for him unless she's witnessing it.

Part of him wants her asleep. Desperately. Because if she's awake, she knows. She knows he's with Kathleen and she knows — because Persephoni always knows — that this is wrong somehow, that something in this yurt is off, and he can't bear her knowing what he is right now. He wants it to be her. He wishes it were her. And if she's awake, that wish is six feet away in the dark, and the distance between what's happening and what he wants is unbearable.

So he doesn't resolve it. He holds both — wanting her awake, wanting her asleep — the way he holds everything about Persephoni: in contradiction, without resolution, passive. Another thing he lets happen to him instead of choosing.

**The look.** There's a moment when Kathleen closes her eyes — lost in it, her head tipped back, not watching him — and he opens his. He looks at the other bed. Persephoni is facing away from them. Her back, her shape under the comforter, the curls on the pillow. As he watches, she shifts — a small movement, her shoulder adjusting, her body resettling. He can't tell if it's an involuntary movement in sleep or if she's awake and trying not to move. He listens for her breathing. He doesn't hear any. If she were asleep, he'd hear it — the yurt is quiet, just canvas and wind and the wood stove ticking. The absence of breathing means she's either holding it or she's too still to hear. He doesn't know. He'll never know. He closes his eyes before Kathleen opens hers.

### Scene E — After
Kathleen against his chest. Her breathing slowing. She's happy — he can feel it in the way she holds him, the way she presses her face into his neck, the way she whispers something he can barely hear. She's in love and she just made love to the boy she's in love with and this is the best night of her life.

Alejandro opens his eyes.

The moonlight is there. The canvas walls glow silver. The yurt is exactly what it was before — fur rugs, wood stove, two beds. And in the other bed, Persephoni's shape under the comforter. Still. Her back to them, maybe. Or her face to the wall. No way to know if she's awake.

He stares at the ceiling — the wooden crown where the support poles meet — and he thinks about the Master locked in the tower. He thinks about the servant running the kingdom. He thinks about how he now has a new piece of data for his classification system: he knows what it feels like to betray someone while they're holding you.

He doesn't find a metaphor for it. Some things resist translation.

Kathleen is asleep against him. Her breathing is even and warm. Outside the yurt, the festival is quiet — just wind and canvas and somewhere far off, that guitar.

## Alejandro's Voice — Specific Notes

The taxonomic brain vs. the body is the engine of this chapter. His usual mode (classify, label, file) meets something that resists classification. Key registers:

- **Before:** Full Alejandro. Cerebral. Processing the festival intellectually. Working on the McGilchrist metaphor. Thinking about Persephoni's line.
- **During Kathleen's approach:** The brain starts losing. Sensation floods in. Technical vocabulary becomes increasingly inadequate. He reaches for clinical terms (arousal, neurochemistry) and they dissolve on contact with the actual experience.
- **During sex:** Oscillation between body (no words, pure sensation) and horrifying taxonomic clarity (observations he doesn't want). The Emissary narrating even now — projecting Persephoni, classifying Kathleen's body against the body it wants, cataloging its own betrayal in real time.
- **After:** Quiet. The brain has nothing to say. The classification system has encountered something it can't file. He stares at the ceiling and doesn't think in words.
