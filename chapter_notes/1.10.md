# Chapter 1.10 — The Updated Model

**POV:** Sergey (third person limited)
**Setting:** Sergey's bedroom studio → FaceTime → school (briefly, post-lockdown easing) → absence
**Time span:** The morning after through the following weeks. Late September–October 2020.
**Timeline:** Immediately follows 1.9. Sergey is 17 (born September 3, 2003).

**Epigraph:** "It had something to do with the rain / Leaching loamy dirt / And the way the back lane came alive / Half moon whispered, 'Go'" — The Weakerthans, "Virtue the Cat Explains Her Departure"

**Prose status:** First draft complete

---

## What This Chapter Does

1. Opens on the other side of the bed — the same moment that ends 1.9, but from inside Sergey's head. She's asleep. The blue light of the monitors. The loop station still humming. "Stone-HENGE" still loaded on the 505. The Emissary wakes up with everything he's wanted, and his first instinct is to be gentle with it.
2. The morning after: Sergey is tender, careful, genuinely moved. He doesn't experience triumph or conquest — he experiences *arrival.* The hypothesis confirmed, but not clinically. He has wanted her since she first opened her mouth and the thing that came out reorganized his entire framework — since before England, since the wanting was a background process he couldn't kill — and now she's here, in his bed, and the wanting has resolved into something warm and quiet and real. He misreads the aftermath as a beginning.
3. The tenderness is the tragedy. He's not callous. He strokes her hair. He thinks about what to say when she wakes up. He is — in his own framework — being the best version of himself. The Emissary at his most human, his most gentle, his most wrong.
4. He tells Kathleen. Not as a confession — as an *update.* The Emissary presenting the revised model. He frames it as honesty, as the right thing, as respect. "I have to tell you something because I respect you too much to lie." But the telling is the cruelest act in the chapter because it serves *his* narrative, not Kathleen's. He's not asking for forgiveness. He's announcing the new configuration.
5. Kathleen's response is quiet devastation. The exclamation marks stop. The warmth doesn't rage or weep — it just goes out. She goes silent in a way that Sergey, from his POV, can only observe from the outside. He sees her face on the screen and it's a face he's never seen before — not the big brown eyes, not the cheerful support, not "that's amazing, babe." Something closed. Something that used to be a door and is now a wall.
6. Kathleen disappears. Not dramatically — she doesn't announce it, doesn't make a scene, doesn't post about it. She just stops. The texts stop. The FaceTime calls go unanswered. The Instagram stories stop. The exclamation marks that were the most consistent thing in both their lives simply end. In lockdown, disappearing is easy — you just stop responding, and the screen that connected you becomes the wall that seals you off. From Sergey's POV, the absence arrives as a fact he can observe but not fully process. She's gone the way a sound is gone — the room is quieter and you can't remember exactly when the sound stopped.

## The Core Tragedy

The chapter's tragedy is not cruelty. It's care.

Sergey is *careful* with Persephone the morning after. He is *honest* with Kathleen. He is *respectful* and *direct.* He does everything right — by the Emissary's standards. He gathers his data (Persephone responded to me), builds his model (we belong together), and presents his findings (I have to tell you something). The process is clean. The conclusion is defensible. Every step follows logically from the step before.

And every step is wrong.

The care is built on a misread. The honesty serves a false narrative. The respect is a frame — "I respect you too much to lie" is a sentence that makes Sergey the protagonist of a story about integrity when the actual story is about a boy who wanted a girl so badly he read her relief as desire and then announced his reading as fact.

He's not lying to Kathleen. That's the worst part. He believes what he's telling her. "Persephone and I — something happened. It's been building for a long time. I think we both felt it." Every word is true *from inside his framework.* The Emissary's report is internally consistent. It's just not what happened.

What happened: a lonely girl felt seen and the seeing overwhelmed her body's ability to distinguish being known from being wanted. What Sergey reports: two people finally acknowledged what they'd both been feeling. Same event. Two frameworks. Only one gets told to Kathleen.

## The Morning After — Inside Sergey's Head

### Waking Up

He wakes before she does. The monitors are still on — the blue glow, his color, the room still his territory. The loop station's display shows the last loaded track: "Stone-HENGE." The headphones are on the floor where they slipped off the desk. The room smells different — her shampoo, her warmth, a scent that doesn't belong to the catalogue of smells this room has always had (solder, cable insulation, the slightly chemical smell of a laptop that runs too hot).

She's asleep. Her curls on his pillow. The golden brown of her skin in the blue light — his color on her skin, the Emissary's wavelength washing over the Master's warmth. He watches her breathe and the watching is not clinical. This is not the boy who classified Kathleen's features in a mental checklist, who focused on the bottom and the eyes because those were the features he could find attractive. This watching is the opposite of classification. He doesn't name what he sees. He just looks. The Emissary, for a moment, gathering instead of grasping.

But only for a moment. Because the mind that classifies everything can't not classify this. And the classification that arrives, slowly, as she breathes and the blue light shifts and the loop station hums, is: *she chose me.*

### The Tenderness

He doesn't wake her. He lies still. He thinks about what to say. He rehearses openings — not manipulatively, not strategically, but the way he rehearses everything, the way the Emissary prepares for any encounter by building a script. He wants to get it right. He wants the first words of this — whatever this is, this new thing, this reconfigured geometry — to be worthy of what happened.

He thinks about making coffee. He doesn't have a coffee maker in his room. He thinks about going to the kitchen and his parents being there and having to explain why he's awake at 6 AM on a Saturday with the specific energy of a boy whose life changed overnight. He stays in bed.

He touches her hair. Lightly. One curl, wound around his finger, released. The texture is different from Kathleen's — he catches himself making the comparison and stops, but not before the data registers. Everything registers. The Emissary can't not catalog.

### What He Believes

He believes this has been building since the beginning. Since before England — since the first time she said something perceptive at the lunch table and his whole system stuttered. The castle didn't start the wanting; it made the wanting impossible to file. The princess riff stripped the last pretense. But the pull was there from the first conversation, the first time her mind arrived at something his mind was still building a framework for. He has managed this wanting for years — sublimated it into music, channeled it into "Careless" and "Enough to See By" and every song that was secretly about her. The wanting was always there. The system held it in place. And last night the system broke and the wanting came through and — here's the crucial part, the misread that will define everything that follows — *she wanted it too.*

He felt her respond. He saw her tears. He saw her body open. He heard her stop breathing when the song landed. And every piece of data points to the same conclusion: she has been wanting this as long as he has, and the song gave them both permission to stop pretending.

The data is accurate. The conclusion is wrong. She responded to being seen, not to wanting him. But from inside the Emissary's framework, the two are indistinguishable — because the Emissary has never experienced being seen without wanting something from the seeing.

## Telling Kathleen

### The Decision

He decides to tell her the same day. This is, in his framework, the honorable thing. The courageous thing. He has read McGilchrist, he has thought about integrity, he knows that hiding this from Kathleen would be the Emissary's worst instinct — the instinct to manage, to control, to keep the system running smoothly by concealing the data that would break it. He won't do that. He's better than that.

The irony: the decision to tell Kathleen is the Emissary at its most self-righteous. He frames the telling as integrity when it's actually *installation* — installing the new narrative, making the new configuration official. He's not confessing. He's announcing. The difference between "I did something terrible" and "something happened" is the entire distance between guilt and declaration. Sergey says "something happened."

### The FaceTime Call

He calls Kathleen on FaceTime. She answers the way she always answers — the big round face filling the screen, the wide eyes, the immediate smile. "Hey, babe." The warmth of her, the Kathleen-ness of her, arriving through the screen the way it always arrives: total, unguarded, present.

He can see her bedroom behind her. The lockdown bedroom — posters, the lamp her parents bought her, a normal teenager's room. Not a studio. Not a production space. Just a room where a girl lives, a girl who has been locked inside by parents who check the doors, a girl who couldn't climb out the window the way Persephone could because her parents were paying attention.

"I need to tell you something."

Her face changes. Not dramatically — a micro-shift, something around the eyes. Her big eyes getting fractionally wider. She knows. She doesn't know *what*, but she knows the register. She's heard "I need to tell you something" before, from other people, in other contexts, and the sentence has never preceded good news. Her body knows before her mind does.

He tells her. "Persephone and I — something happened. Last night. It's been building for a while and I think — I think we both felt it. I'm telling you because I respect you too much to lie about it."

He says "I respect you too much to lie" and he means it. That's the devastating part. He means every word. The Emissary's presentation is sincere. He is genuinely trying to do the right thing, genuinely trying to be honest, genuinely trying to show Kathleen the respect of truth. He doesn't see that the truth he's presenting is built on a foundation of misread data. He doesn't see that "we both felt it" is his interpretation, not Persephone's. He doesn't see that "it's been building" is his narrative arc, imposed on months of creative partnership that felt like something else from the other side.

He tells her the story of last night the way the Emissary tells every story: structured, sequential, building toward a conclusion that was always implicit in the premises. He played her the song. She responded. The response was real. The conclusion follows.

### Kathleen's Response

She doesn't scream. She doesn't cry. She doesn't say "how could you."

She goes quiet.

The face on the screen — the round face, the wide eyes, the girl who said "that's amazing, babe" with exclamation marks that never wavered — goes still. Not the stillness of shock. Something deeper. The stillness of a thing finishing. The way a song ends: not with silence but with the absence of sound, which is different, which has a shape, which is the outline of everything that was there a moment ago.

She looks at him through the screen. Her eyes. The same eyes that watched him play "Enough to See By" and heard something beautiful and missed the yurt. The same eyes that watched Persephone's TikToks and said "perfect" and held the phone and bounced on the bed when the numbers climbed. The same eyes that looked at him in England and chose him and stayed and stayed and stayed.

She says something. Something small. From Sergey's POV, he can hear the words but can't fully hear them — the way you can see a building collapse on a screen but can't feel the ground shake. Whatever she says, it's not loud. It's not dramatic. It's the shortest sentence she's ever spoken to him, shorter than "that's beautiful, babe," shorter than "shh," shorter than anything she's said in three years of loving him with exclamation marks and patience and a playlist with twelve listeners.

She hangs up.

### After the Call

The screen goes dark. His face reflected in his phone — the blue light of the monitors behind him, the Emissary's color, the room where it happened. He holds the phone and he feels — what? Something he can almost classify. Not guilt, exactly. Not yet. Something more like the sensation of a structure behaving unexpectedly. He had modeled her response. He had expected tears, or anger, or questions. He had prepared for those. He had not prepared for quiet. The quiet is outside the model.

He texts Persephone: *I told Kathleen.*

The text sits there. Three words. The period at the end. He stares at it and the three words look like a conclusion, like the last line of a proof, like Q.E.D. — the thing to be demonstrated has been demonstrated. The old configuration is acknowledged. The new one can begin.

He doesn't hear from Kathleen again.

## Kathleen's Disappearance

### The Exclamation Marks Stop

The first thing Sergey notices is the absence of texts. Not the content of the texts — the *existence* of them. Kathleen texted every day. Multiple times a day. The texts were consistent the way weather was consistent — you stopped noticing them because they were always there. Good morning texts. Song-reaction texts. Selfies in her lockdown bedroom with bored faces and silly filters. "How's the new track?" "Miss you babe." "Tell Persephone I said hi!" The exclamation marks. Always the exclamation marks.

They stop.

Not gradually. Not trailing off. They just stop, the way a heartbeat stops — one moment present, the next moment the most noticeable absence in the world. He wakes up the first morning and there's no good morning text and the phone looks wrong without it, like a face missing a feature.

He texts her. *Hey.* No response. He texts again the next day. *Can we talk?* No response. He tries calling. It rings and rings and the ringing is the loneliest sound he's ever heard — lonelier than his music, lonelier than the twenty people in a room who are all him, because the ringing is a sound meant for two people and one of them isn't there.

### The Disappearance

In lockdown, vanishing requires no logistics. You don't need to move, transfer, pack boxes. You just stop answering. The screen that connected you becomes the wall that seals you off, and the wall is perfect — no cracks, no gaps, no way to press your ear to it and hear what's on the other side.

Her Instagram stops updating. The last post is from before the FaceTime call — a sunset photo from her bedroom window, captioned with a single line, no exclamation marks: *the view from here.*

The Weakerthans echo: Virtue the Cat didn't announce her departure either. She just slipped out the back lane. The half moon whispered *go* and she went. Virtue left because the sound someone found for her — her name, her identity — was lost. Kathleen leaves because the sound someone found for her — "that's amazing, babe," the girl who held the phone, the first listener, the loyal audience — was the wrong sound. The name they gave her was never her name. It was her function.

### What Sergey Observes

From his POV, Kathleen's disappearance is a fact he can document but not fully interpret. He sees the empty locker, the absent name, the unanswered texts. He registers the data. He knows — in the way the Emissary knows things, categorically, structurally — that he caused this. He told her, and she left. Cause and effect. The model is clear.

But he can't access what she's feeling. He's in strict third person limited, and Kathleen's interior is as sealed to him as her locked bedroom door was during lockdown. He can only see the outside: the silence, the absence, the vacancy where a person used to be. And the outside tells him almost nothing, because Kathleen's devastation is quiet, and quiet devastation is the one thing the Emissary can't classify. Loud grief has data points — tears, words, volume. Quiet grief is the absence of data. It's a graph with nothing on it. It's forty monthly listeners going to zero.

He feels something about this that he cannot name. The Emissary has categories for most things — guilt, regret, loss, relief, sorrow — and none of them fit precisely, the way none of his early songs fit precisely, the way the framework always almost but never quite contained the feeling. What he feels is the specific discomfort of a system that achieved its stated objective and produced an outcome the system didn't model. He got what he wanted. The cost was a person. The cost was the girl who said "that's beautiful, babe" and meant it every time and is now a silence on the other end of a phone that rings and rings.

He should feel worse than he does. He knows this. The knowledge that he should feel worse is itself a feeling — a meta-guilt, a guilt about insufficient guilt — and the Emissary classifies this too, files it, notes it, moves on. Because Persephone is in his bed now. Persephone texts him back. Persephone is the revised model, the updated configuration, the thing the system was always pointed toward, and the thing the system gave up to get there is already becoming an absence he'll learn to live inside the way you learn to live inside any room with a missing wall — by not looking at the gap.

## Persephone in the Aftermath

### What Sergey Sees

From his POV, Persephone after the betrayal is complicated in ways he can almost but not quite parse. She's present — she's in his room, she's in his bed, she's texting, she's coming over late at night the way she was before, but the before and the after are different in ways the Emissary can detect but not diagnose.

She's quieter. Not Kathleen-quiet — not the quiet of a thing ending — but the quiet of a person carrying something heavy and not mentioning it. She still laughs. She still does the Sheepey voice. She still makes observations that cut through his entire framework with a single sentence. But there's a layer now — something underneath the surface that wasn't there before, or was there and he couldn't see it, and now he can see it because she's closer and closer means more data and more data means more he can't explain.

She flinches when Kathleen's name comes up. Not physically — not a visible recoil — but something happens behind her eyes, a micro-shift, the green going fractionally darker, and Sergey catalogs this without understanding it. He thinks she feels guilty. He's right — she does — but the guilt he imagines is not the guilt she carries. He imagines the guilt of choosing one person over another. She carries the guilt of not choosing at all — of being overtaken by loneliness and letting it wash her into a bed she never decided to be in.

He is gentle with her about Kathleen. He says things like "she'll be okay." But the bigger narratives — "it was going to happen eventually," "we couldn't keep pretending" — he thinks them without saying them. These sentences live in his head, not in conversation, because on some level the Emissary knows not to submit them for confirmation. He knows that if he says "it was going to happen eventually" she will look at him in a way that breaks the model. He knows that "we couldn't keep pretending" requires a *we* that she hasn't offered. So the frameworks stay internal — the Emissary's analgesic, self-administered, never tested against the other person's reality. He believes them. He needs to believe them. The alternative — that what happened wasn't inevitable, wasn't mutual, wasn't a revelation but a misread — is outside the model, and outside the model is where the Emissary cannot go. So he doesn't ask. The Emissary doesn't run experiments whose results might falsify the hypothesis.

### The Sheepey Problem

Something specific Sergey notices but misreads: Persephone stops doing the Sheepey voice for a while. Days go by without Sheepey. The drunk sheep from Stonehenge — the character that was the purest expression of Persephone's creative joy, the thing she and Kathleen built together in the car, the voice that "Stone-HENGE" was written about — goes quiet.

Sergey notices the absence but reads it wrong. He thinks she's maturing past it. Growing up. Leaving the childish thing behind as the adult thing begins. The Emissary's reading: Sheepey was the girl's toy; the woman doesn't need it.

What's actually happening: Sheepey was Kathleen's too. The voice belonged to both of them — Persephone invented it, Kathleen played the straight man, and the character lived in the space between them. With Kathleen gone, Sheepey is a duet with a missing voice. Persephone can't do Sheepey alone the way she used to because the comedy was always a conversation, and the other person in the conversation has stopped answering.

The sheep who gained a name in "Stone-HENGE" is losing something else — the context that gave the name meaning. Virtue the Cat lost "the sound that you found for me." Sheepey is losing the audience that made the sound worth making.

## Threads Planted

- **The misread as the foundation.** Everything that follows — the famous couple of 1.11, the Emissary with everything he wanted in 1.12 — is built on Sergey's reading of that night. He thinks she chose him. She was drowning. The relationship begins inside a framework that doesn't match the feeling, and neither of them can say so because she doesn't have the language yet and he has too much language and all of it is wrong.
- **Kathleen gone forever.** She doesn't come back. She doesn't resurface in Act 1. The girl who was the Master — intuitive, relational, present, the warm hand on his arm — is gone from the story the way the Master is gone from the Emissary's world: not destroyed but abandoned, not dead but unreachable. The triangle is a line now. Two points. The line can go a long way but it can't enclose anything.
- **The gaslighting chain completed.** George narrated Rosemary. Sergey narrated Kathleen. Now Sergey narrates Persephone — "we both felt it," the Emissary overwriting the Master's experience with his framework. Same mechanism. The chain: George → Rosemary, Sergey → Kathleen, Sergey → Persephone. The Emissary's certainty applied to other people's interiors.
- **Sheepey's silence.** The character going quiet foreshadows the larger loss — not just Kathleen but the part of Persephone that could play without consequence, create without audience, be silly without strategy. Sheepey will come back, but changed. The innocence is gone.
- **"She'll be okay."** Sergey's analgesic — the framework applied to Kathleen's pain. He doesn't know if she'll be okay. He needs to believe she'll be okay because the alternative makes him the villain and the Emissary cannot be the villain of his own story. This sentence will echo when Persephone cheats on *him* in 1.13 and nobody says "he'll be okay" because by then nobody is left to say it.
- **The phone that rings and rings.** Sergey calling Kathleen and getting no answer. The loneliest sound — a sound designed for two people with only one person present. This echoes forward to his own isolation in 1.14, when the mirror turns and he's the one the phone doesn't reach.

## What This Chapter Is NOT

- It's not Kathleen's chapter. Her interiority is sealed. From Sergey's POV, we see her face on the screen and her absence afterward, but we never enter her head. The devastation is inferred from observable behavior — the silence, the unanswered calls, the exclamation marks ending. This is the cruelty of third person limited applied to the person most wronged: we can't access her pain because the person through whose eyes we're seeing can't access it either.
- It's not a villain origin story. Sergey is not cruel. He's tender, careful, sincere — and completely, catastrophically wrong. The tenderness is real. The care is real. The reading is false. The chapter should make the reader ache for Kathleen while understanding exactly how Sergey got here.
- It's not about sex. The physical relationship with Persephone is present but not the point. The point is the narrative Sergey builds around the physical — the love story he constructs from the data, the origin myth he's already writing, the framework that turns a misread into a romance.
- It's not the end of the triangle. The triangle ended when Kathleen hung up. This chapter is the aftermath — the silence after the song stops, the room after the sound leaves, the screen that stays dark and the phone that rings and rings in a bedroom across town where nobody answers.

## Scene Breakdown

### Scene A — The Other Side of the Bed
The opening. Same moment as the end of 1.9, but from Sergey's perspective. Blue light. Monitors. Loop station humming. Persephone asleep. The tenderness. The catalog of sensory details — her hair, her scent, the warmth of her against the cool blue room. The Emissary waking up inside the moment and, for once, not rushing to classify it. The gathering that lasts a moment before the grasping begins: *she chose me.*

### Scene B — The Morning
She wakes up. The first interaction. Awkward and tender in the way all mornings-after are for seventeen-year-olds — uncertain bodies, uncertain words, the gap between the magnitude of what happened and the smallness of "do you want water?" or "what time is it?" Sergey is careful. Persephone is — something. Quiet but not distant. Present but carrying something he can see but can't name. He reads her quietness as the awe of the new. She is carrying something else entirely.

### Scene C — The FaceTime Call
The telling. Sergey calls Kathleen. The setup: his decision, his framing, his preparation. Then the call itself — Kathleen's face on the screen, the smile, the "hey babe," the shift when he says "I need to tell you something." The words he uses. The sincerity of the words. The wrongness underneath the sincerity. Her response: the quiet. The closing. The stillness that is not any stillness he's seen before. The screen going dark.

### Scene D — The Silence
The days after. The texts that don't arrive. The phone that rings. The Instagram that stops. In lockdown, absence is digital — not an empty chair but an unanswered text, not a cleared locker but a screen that stays dark. Sergey navigating the absence — cataloging it, trying to file it, finding that the categories don't fit. The meta-guilt: knowing he should feel worse, feeling guilty about feeling insufficient guilt. Meanwhile, Persephone is there, and being with Persephone is the thing he wanted, and the wanting-fulfilled is supposed to feel like arrival but instead feels like standing in a room where a wall is missing.

### Scene E — The New Configuration
Late in the chapter. Sergey and Persephone together in his studio, the way they were before, except now they're *together* together, and the togetherness changes the air in the room. The music continues. The Instagram strategy continues. The creative partnership is now also a romantic relationship, and the romantic relationship sits on top of the creative partnership the way a second story sits on a first — same foundation, new view. He notices Sheepey's absence. He misreads it. He's gentle about Kathleen. He says "she'll be okay." The chapter ends with him in his studio, alone, the loop station on, the blue light, building a new track — and underneath the building, underneath the loops and the layers and the architecture of sound, the faintest awareness that something is wrong with the model. Not wrong enough to rebuild. Just wrong enough to notice. A draft in a room where a wall used to be.
