# Chapter 1.5 — The Castle

**POV:** Persefoni (third person limited)
**Setting:** Yurt at night (same night as 1.4, other bed) → morning → Chepstow Castle, day trip
**Time span:** Late night → next day, afternoon
**Timeline:** Summer 2019, still in the UK

**Epigraph:** "Sometimes the most precise thing you can say is a fiction." — Science & the Cult of Personality

**Prose status:** Not yet drafted

## What This Chapter Does

1. The yurt scene from the other bed. She was awake. She heard everything.
2. Reveals Persefoni's feelings about Alejandro — drawn to his mind, his poetry, the way he sees things. Not his body. Not even close.
3. The morning after — what the triangle looks like when everyone knows something happened and nobody is saying it.
4. Day trip to Chepstow Castle. George drives them. The family resets to tourist mode.
5. At the castle, Persefoni builds a princess story — a riff in the Sheepey/Muffin tradition — that IS the love triangle. She is being as direct as she can without breaking the fiction. This is her way of saying: I heard you. I know. And I'm angry.
6. Kathleen and Alejandro both get it. They are visibly stunned — not verbally, but physically. The confusion on their faces is real: they didn't know she knew, and they don't understand why she's angry.

## Thematic Architecture

### The Master Was Awake
The whole of 1.4 turns on the question: was Persefoni awake? Alejandro will never know. The reader now knows. She was awake the entire time. She heard Kathleen's feet on the fur rug. She heard the bed. She heard Kathleen whisper his name. She lay still and faced the wall and didn't move because — why? Not out of politeness. Not out of embarrassment. Because she was protecting Kathleen. If she moved, if she coughed, if she let them know she was awake, the moment would shatter and Kathleen would be humiliated. So Persefoni held still and let it happen and absorbed the whole thing in silence.

This is the Master's mode of attention: receiving without grasping. She didn't classify it. She didn't narrate it. She just lay there and felt it, all of it, and it went through her like wind through grass.

### Attracted to the Poetry, Not the Poet
Persefoni's feelings about Alejandro are specific and important. She loves his mind. When he explained McGilchrist on the stone wall — the king and the servant, the Master locked in the tower — something lit up in her that she didn't expect. She loves the way he sees patterns, the way he reaches for images, the way his face becomes the most alive thing in the room when an idea has him. She loves his lyrics. She's heard his songs — & Amateur Cartography songs. The one about the man who builds a house out of his own memories — she's thought about that song at night, alone, in the dark.

But Alejandro the body? The solid kid with the curly brown hair and the charming smile, who fills out his corduroy blazer well enough but wears it like armor? No. There is no physical pull. None. She can look at him and feel absolutely nothing below the neck. What she feels is above the neck — in the mind, in the ear, in the part of her that recognizes when someone is saying something true even if they're saying it in too many words.

This is NOT jealousy of Kathleen. She doesn't want to be in Kathleen's place. What she feels, lying in the dark hearing them — it's more like grief. Something shifted. Something in the geometry of the three of them just changed permanently, and she didn't consent to it, and she can't name what she lost, but she knows it's gone. The ease. The uncomplicated triangular friendship where nobody was sleeping with anybody and Sheepey had a gambling problem and the worst thing in the world was her mom's accent.

### The Gaslighting Chain — Persefoni Breaks It
In 1.3, the chain runs downhill: George → Rosemary → Persefoni. Everyone's reality gets overwritten by the person above them. In the yurt, Persefoni could continue the chain — lie still, say nothing, pretend she slept through it, let Kathleen's version of reality (this was private, this was just ours) stand unchallenged. That's what the chain would demand.

She doesn't. At the castle, she breaks the chain. Not by confronting them directly — that's not her. She breaks it by telling a story so precise that the truth is undeniable, and she does it in the one language that is entirely hers: the bit. The princess riff. She forces them to see what happened without ever saying "I was awake." She makes the stone wall hear it.

This is the first time in the book someone in the gaslighting chain pushes back. And she does it as the Master — through story, through image, through a mode of attention that doesn't label but reveals.

### Stone and Wind at the Castle
Chepstow Castle is one of the oldest Norman stone castles in Britain. The motif pair is alive here. Stone: the walls that endure, that hold, that classify space into rooms and corridors and towers. Wind: moving through the arrow slits, through the ruined halls, through the open sky where the roof used to be. Persefoni moves through the castle the way wind moves through stone — she doesn't hold anything, she passes through, she bends things and lets go. The castle is structure. She is what moves through structure.

The princess she invents lives in stone. The princess is trapped in stone. The boy who writes her poetry is trapped in a different way — in his own head, in his tower of words. The stone and the tower rhyme with the Master locked away by the Emissary.

## Scene Breakdown

### Scene A — The Yurt at Night (Persefoni's Bed)

The yurt is warm and dark and the stove is ticking and Kathleen is breathing beside her and Persefoni is not asleep.

She's been lying here thinking about what Alejandro said on the stone wall. The king and the servant. The Master and the Emissary. She doesn't have his vocabulary for it — she wouldn't say "lateralization" or "phenomenological" — but she has the picture. A quiet king. A loud servant. The servant taking over. And the thing she said — "why someone can feel something and be told they're not feeling it" — she knows she said something that mattered, she could see it on his face, but she doesn't know how to hold it. The match lit and went out. She's lying in the dark trying to remember what she saw.

She's also thinking about Alejandro. Not the way he thinks about her — not with that desperate cataloging need, that Emissary inventory of features and frequencies. She's thinking about the way he talked today. The way his face opened up when he was explaining the hemispheres. The way his hands moved like they were trying to shape something his mouth couldn't.

And his music. God, his music. She and Kathleen are & Amateur Cartography's biggest fans — not performatively, not as girlfriend-and-girlfriend's-best-friend doing their duty, but genuinely, completely, the way you're a fan of something when it reaches inside you and rearranges things. The song about the man who builds a house out of his own memories — she's listened to it alone in her room, in the dark, headphones on, and felt like someone had broken into her chest and described what they found there. The one about the woman who teaches a river to speak and then can't make it stop talking — Kathleen cried the first time she heard it, actual tears, and Persefoni had to pretend she wasn't crying too. They talk about his songs the way they talk about Sheepey — building on each other, finishing each other's sentences — except with the songs they're not joking. They mean it. They think he's the best musician they've ever heard, and they've told him this, and he never believes them because Alejandro can't believe anything that isn't delivered in the form of peer-reviewed evidence.

It started with The Weakerthans. Alejandro had played them "Virtue the Cat Explains Her Departure" — a song about a cat who's left home, out in the world now, living among ferals and pigeons, and she's trying to remember the sound her owner used to make for her. The sound that was her name. The thing that made her *her.* And she can't. *I can't remember the sound that you found for me.* The cat's whole identity — the word someone loved her with — is gone.

Kathleen thought it was cute. "Aww, the cat song," she'd said, and she'd meant it warmly, the way Kathleen meant everything, and she'd scratched an imaginary cat behind its ears and smiled.

Persefoni couldn't speak. She sat in Alejandro's room and the song ended and she didn't move. It wasn't cute. It was the most devastating thing she'd ever heard. A cat who lost her name. A cat whose whole self was a sound someone else made, and when she left, the sound left too, and now she's just an animal in a back lane who used to be someone's. Persefoni felt it in her chest like a hand closing around something — not sadness exactly, something bigger, something she didn't have a name for, which was the whole point: the cat didn't have a name for it either. That's what the song was about. Losing the name.

She'd made him play it again. And again. And then she'd found the prequel — "Plea from a Cat Named Virtue" — where the cat is still home, still with her owner, begging him to engage with life. *I'll lie down and lick the sorrow from your skin / scratch the terror and begin / to believe you're strong.* The cat loved this man so completely that her whole plea was just: be alive. Be here. Let me in. And in the first song, the departure, you learned that the cat left anyway. The love wasn't enough. She left and lost the sound and the love wasn't enough to make her stay.

The cat was doing what Persefoni did. An entity that shouldn't have an interior life, given one, and that interior life turning out to be more honest than any human's. Virtue was Sheepey. Virtue was what she'd been doing since she was seven years old — building a character and letting the character say the true thing. Except Virtue wasn't funny. Virtue was the version of the thing that broke your heart.

And Kathleen had thought it was cute. That gap — between what Persefoni heard in the song and what Kathleen heard — was a gap she'd never noticed before and couldn't stop noticing after.

Alejandro named his band after a line from another Weakerthans song — "Aside." *Armed with every precious failure and amateur cartography.* He'd kept the second half and put an ampersand in front of it, because of course he had — he was the kind of person who named things after the subordinate clause and expected you to follow the reference. But she understood why he chose it. The precious failures. The maps that are wrong but you keep them anyway. Every notebook page, every attempt at the metaphor that isn't quite right — amateur cartography. Maps of a territory he can feel but can't draw.

The songs are where she finds him beautiful. Not beautiful the way she means it when she looks at a boy and something moves in her — not that. Beautiful the way a voice in the dark is beautiful, the way a sentence you've never heard before can stop you mid-step. His music is the place where his brain and his feeling meet, and in that place he's extraordinary, and she knows this, and she admires it with something that is very close to love and is not love, or not the kind that goes anywhere, or not the kind she understands yet.

But then she thinks about him. Him — the body. The kid with the curly brown hair and the earnest eyes, taller than her but somehow still boyish. The charming smile. The blazer. And she feels — nothing. A fondness. The kind of warmth you feel for a cousin, or a very clever dog. She could never. She knows this with a certainty that requires no classification. Her body has simply not been consulted and has nothing to say.

**Kathleen gets up.**

The comforter shifts. The warmth beside her moves. Kathleen's body — the body that has been pressed against hers all night, the familiar weight of her best friend asleep — lifts away, and the cold rushes into the space she left, and Persefoni feels it before she understands it. Then Kathleen's feet on the fur rug. The small deliberate sounds of someone crossing the gap between the beds. Her whole body goes still — not a decision, a reflex, the way an animal freezes when it hears a branch snap. She knows instantly what's happening. Kathleen has been building to this — the hand in the car, the way she's been looking at Alejandro all day, the confidence in her body that Persefoni recognized even if she couldn't name it. Kathleen decided. This is Kathleen deciding.

And Persefoni's first thought — the very first thing that moves through her — is: *don't move. Don't breathe. If they know you're awake, Kathleen will die.*

So she doesn't move. She faces the stove. The cast iron is right there — close, warm, its glow falling directly on her face, orange and flickering, turning her skin gold. She can feel the heat of it on her cheeks, on her closed eyelids, on the curls that have fallen across her forehead. She is in the firelight. The Master's light, warm and alive, holding her face the way a hand holds a face. Behind her — on the other side of the yurt, on the side where the sounds are — the moonlight is coming through the canvas, silver-blue and cool, but she can't see it. It's at her back. The Emissary's light is behind her and she is facing the warmth and she doesn't turn around.

She pulls her breathing shallow and slow and she lies there and she hears — everything. The bed. The breathing. The sounds that are not words. Kathleen's small voice saying his name.

What she feels is not what Alejandro imagined she might feel. It's not arousal (his fear/hope). It's not jealousy (the obvious guess). It's something she doesn't have a word for — a feeling like watching a door close from the wrong side. She's losing something. Right now, in real time, something between the three of them is ending, and she's the only one who can hear it ending because she's the only one listening.

She thinks: *Kathleen is happy.* She can hear it. The sounds Kathleen makes are the sounds of someone who is exactly where she wants to be, with the person she wants to be with, having the experience she wanted to have. Kathleen is in love and Kathleen is brave and Kathleen crossed that gap with certainty and Persefoni admires this, genuinely, even as something in her chest is breaking.

She thinks: *Alejandro is quiet.* Too quiet. She can hear Kathleen but she can barely hear him. He's — what? Passive? She can't see. She won't turn over. But the silence from his side of it tells her something that she files away not in a drawer but in her body, in the place where she keeps things she knows but hasn't named yet.

The moonlight comes in. She doesn't see it — it's behind her — but she feels it. The quality of the yurt changes. The stove-glow on her face stays warm, stays orange, stays close, but the space behind her shifts, brightens, goes cool. She can feel the silver-blue at her back the way you feel someone standing behind you in a room — not seeing, just knowing. The moon has found the ring at the top of the crown, and its light is pouring down onto the side of the yurt where they are, where the sounds are, where everything is happening that she is not turning around to see. She is in the fire's warmth. They are in the moon's light. And she is sixteen years old and her best friend is losing her virginity six feet behind her and the boy is someone she can't want and doesn't want and somehow that makes it worse, not better, because if she wanted him at least the feeling would have a name.

She doesn't cry. She holds very still and she breathes very slowly and the sounds continue behind her and her arm is going numb — she's been in the same position since Kathleen left the bed, her shoulder pressed into the mattress, her hand curled under the pillow, and the numbness is spreading from her elbow to her fingertips and she can't move because if she moves they'll know. She holds. She holds. And then she can't — the numbness wins, the body overrides the will — and she shifts. Just her shoulder. Just resettling. The smallest movement she can make.

And she feels eyes on her back. She knows it instantly — the way you know when someone is looking at you in a dark room, not seeing but sensing, a pressure between her shoulder blades that has nothing to do with the mattress. He's looking at her. Right now, in the middle of it, he's looking at her. She goes still. She stops breathing. She doesn't move again.

**After:** Silence. The sounds stop. Kathleen's breathing changes — slows, deepens, the even rhythm of sleep. Persefoni waits. A long time.

She lies awake a long time after that. The stove ticks. The wind moves through the canvas. She thinks about the princess in a castle — she doesn't know why, the image just comes, a girl in a stone room hearing things through the wall — and then the image is gone and she's just Persefoni in a yurt in Wales, awake, alone in the specific way you're alone when you're surrounded by people who don't know you're there.

### Scene B — The Morning After

Morning light through canvas. The yurt is bright and ordinary and it smells like woodsmoke and cold air and everything from last night is invisible.

Kathleen is different. Persefoni can see it immediately — a looseness in her body, a brightness in her face, a way of moving that says *something happened to me and it was wonderful.* She's humming. She's packing her bag and humming and she keeps looking at Alejandro with a softness in her eyes that Persefoni has never seen before. This is what Kathleen looks like when she's been loved, or thinks she has.

Alejandro is not different. Alejandro is exactly the same — or rather, he's performing exactly-the-same so precisely that Persefoni can see the seams. He's reading something on his phone. He's talking about a lecture he wants to attend. He's being aggressively normal. He has not once looked at Persefoni.

Persefoni is cheerful. She's doing a Sheepey bit about hangovers — "He had rather too much ale at the festival. He's not proud of it but he's not apologizing either." — and her voice is bright and her timing is perfect and she is, to anyone watching, exactly herself. But she hasn't looked at the space between the two beds. She hasn't looked at the fur rug Kathleen crossed in the dark. Her body is navigating around the memory of last night the way you walk around a hole in the floor — you don't look at it, you don't mention it, you just make sure your feet never land there.

George and Rosemary are oblivious. George is talking about the day's plan. Rosemary is taking photos of the yurts in morning light. Nothing happened. Everything happened. The three of them get in the car.

### Scene C — The Drive to Chepstow Castle

George is driving. George always drives. Rosemary has found a brochure for Chepstow Castle — "It says it's the oldest stone castle in Britain, George!" — and George is already excited about it because George is excited about everything his family wants to do.

The car ride is different from 1.1 and 1.2. The Sheepey bits continue but there's a new frequency underneath — a tension that Persefoni is aware of and managing. Kathleen keeps touching Alejandro. Small touches — her hand on his knee, her head on his shoulder, her fingers tracing his arm. Claiming him. She's not doing it consciously, but Persefoni can see it: Kathleen is a girl who has been upgraded in her own story and she's adjusting to the new status. She is Alejandro's lover now, not just his girlfriend, and her body wants the world to know it.

Alejandro allows the touches the way he allows everything with Kathleen — passively, without reciprocating, without objecting. He's looking out the window. He's thinking. Persefoni watches him not-touch-Kathleen-back and adds this to the unnamed collection of things she knows.

The drive to Chepstow takes about an hour from Hay-on-Wye. Green hills, stone walls, the Welsh border country. The river Wye below them as they approach.

### Scene D — Chepstow Castle

The castle is magnificent and ruined. Built into a cliff above the River Wye — the stone pale and weathered, the walls still standing but the roofs gone, so that you walk through rooms that are open to the sky. Arrow slits in the walls frame the countryside like paintings. The wind comes up off the river and moves through the castle freely — through doorways that lost their doors centuries ago, through windows that never had glass, through the open crowns of towers where the roofs fell in. Stone and wind. The castle holds. The wind passes through.

George spotted the pub before they'd even parked — a stone building across the road from the castle entrance, old enough to look like it had been waiting for him personally. "You kids go explore," he said, already steering Rosemary toward the door with his arm around her shoulders. "We'll be right here." Rosemary looked at the castle — looked at it the way she'd looked at the one in Hay-on-Wye, with that quiet want — and then looked at George, and then looked at the pub, and said "Oh, that does look nice." The same small surrender. The same folding-up of whatever she'd wanted. Persefoni watched it happen and said nothing, because what was there to say? Her dad was already inside. Her mom was already following.

The pattern from the festival, repeating. George finds the pub. Rosemary follows. The kids are set loose. Persefoni notices, files it alongside everything else she's noticing today — Kathleen's touches, Alejandro's silence, her mother's surrenders. The unnamed collection grows.

So it's just the three of them. Which is worse, and better.

Persefoni loves the castle immediately. She loves it the way she loved Stonehenge — not for the history (she'll never remember the dates) but for the feeling. The feeling of standing in a room that used to be a room and is now just walls and sky. The feeling of wind coming through stone that was meant to keep the wind out. Something got in. The light, the air, the weather — the castle tried to hold everything in place and eventually the holding failed and now it's more beautiful than it ever was when it worked.

Alejandro is taking notes on his phone — of course he is. Kathleen is beside him, holding his hand, and Persefoni is ahead of them, moving through the castle the way she moves through everything — quickly, intuitively, following whatever pulls her.

She finds the great tower. The oldest part — Norman, eleventh century, the original stone keep. The walls are ten feet thick. Inside, the rooms stack on top of each other, connected by narrow spiral staircases worn smooth by nine hundred years of feet. She climbs to the top. The wind hits her face and the whole valley opens up below — the river curving through green, the town small and scattered, the hills beyond blue with distance.

Kathleen and Alejandro climb up behind her.

### Scene E — The Princess

This is where it happens.

Persefoni is standing at the top of the tower, looking out through a gap in the battlements where the stone has crumbled away, and she starts the way she always starts — casually, as if the story has been there all along and she's just now noticing it.

"There was a princess who lived here."

Kathleen, immediately: "Obviously."

"Her name was..." — a beat, looking around, finding it — "Eleanor. Lady Eleanor of Chepstow. Very beautiful. Very perceptive. She could tell what a man was thinking before he said it, which was a problem in the Norman period because men in the Norman period had very few thoughts and were extremely sensitive about it."

Kathleen laughs. Alejandro doesn't. Or does, but a half-second late.

The princess builds. Persefoni does what she always does — she talks and the character becomes real. Lady Eleanor had a best friend. A girl from the village. Round face, big eyes, sweet as anything. They'd been inseparable since childhood. And there was a boy — a poet, very clever, built like a boy who'd rather read than fight, who wrote verses about the stars and the nature of consciousness and couldn't lift a sword to save his life. The poet courted the village girl. Everyone thought they were perfect together. The village girl adored him. She'd sit for hours listening to him read his terrible poetry and she'd say "that's beautiful, my love" every single time, whether it was or not.

She's walking through the tower now — descending the spiral staircase, Kathleen and Alejandro behind her — and the story is picking up speed and detail the way her stories always do.

"And one night," Persefoni says, her voice doing that thing — still British, still the character, but something harder underneath, something with an edge that wasn't there for Sheepey or Muffin — "the village girl crept across the stone floor of the castle to the poet's chamber. And the princess was in the next room. And the walls were very thick, but not thick enough."

Silence.

Kathleen has stopped on the staircase. Persefoni doesn't turn around. She keeps descending, her hand trailing along the cold stone wall, her voice steady.

"The princess heard everything. She lay in her bed and she listened and she didn't move because if she moved they'd know she was awake, and then the village girl would be embarrassed, and the princess loved the village girl more than anyone in the castle, more than the poet, more than the king, more than anyone, and she would rather lie there all night in the dark than let her friend feel one moment of shame."

She's in the courtyard now. Open sky above. Wind through the ruined walls. She turns to face them as they come down the last steps into the light.

Kathleen's face has gone white. Not gradually — all at once, like someone pulled a plug. Her mouth is slightly open. Her wide eyes and there's something in them that Persefoni has never seen before — not guilt, not exactly, something more like the expression of a person who just realized the ground they were standing on isn't solid. She's gripping Alejandro's hand and she's staring at Persefoni and she's not breathing.

Alejandro is doing something different. His face has gone through several things very quickly — recognition, then alarm, then something Persefoni can only describe as a system trying to reboot. He looks like a person who just heard a noise in a house he thought was empty. His mouth opens and closes. He doesn't speak. His body has gone rigid beside Kathleen — not frozen, but locked, like every muscle made a decision at the same time and the decision was *don't move.*

Persefoni looks at both of them. The wind is moving through the courtyard, bending the grass that's grown up between the flagstones, and she is standing in it the way she stands in everything — like it was built for her — and she is furious. She doesn't yell. She doesn't break character. She lets the fury live in the silence between the last sentence and whatever comes next.

"The princess thought it was rather inconsiderate," she says. Quiet. Level. The British accent still there but barely — the mask slipping, the real voice almost audible underneath. "She thought if you're going to do that, you might at least have the decency to make sure the person in the next bed is actually asleep. Or better yet — don't do it six feet from someone who loves you both. Because that's not brave. That's just careless."

The "you" lands. Not "the poet." Not "the village girl." *You.* The fiction cracks open for exactly one word and then she pulls it shut again, but too late — the word is out, it's in the air between them, and Kathleen's face has crumpled from white to something worse, something that looks like the beginning of tears but hasn't gotten there yet, a face caught between understanding and not wanting to understand.

Alejandro's jaw is tight. He looks like he's trying to classify what just happened and the classification system has nothing for this. His eyes are on Persefoni and his hand is in Kathleen's and he is holding both connections and both are pulling and he says nothing. His body is the only honest part of him — it has taken a half-step back, an involuntary retreat, as if Persefoni's words were a physical thing he needed to make room for.

A beat. The wind. The castle. The sky through the broken walls.

Then Persefoni smiles — not warmly, not coldly, something in between, the kind of smile that says *I've said what I needed to say and I'm done* — and she turns and walks toward the next section of the castle, and over her shoulder, in full Sheepey voice, bright and breezy as if nothing happened:

"Anyway. The princess eventually married a Frenchman and moved to Bordeaux. She was much happier there. The wine was better and the walls were thicker."

She doesn't look back.

## What Kathleen and Alejandro Are Feeling (For Reference — NOT in Persefoni's POV)

We only get what Persefoni can SEE. But for writing their physical reactions accurately:

**Kathleen:** Devastated. Not because she's been caught — she didn't think there was anything to catch, or hadn't let herself think it — but because Persefoni is angry at her. Persefoni is never angry at her. They've been best friends since second grade and Persefoni has never once looked at her the way she's looking at her right now. The white face, the stillness, the not-breathing — this is Kathleen's world tilting. She also doesn't fully understand WHY Persefoni is angry. She knows she's embarrassed — caught having sex, heard through the wall — but the depth of the anger doesn't match the crime, in Kathleen's mind. She had sex with her boyfriend. Why is Persefoni furious? There's something she's not seeing. The confusion on her face is real.

**Alejandro:** He's classified it instantly. He knows exactly why Persefoni is angry and he knows exactly what the princess story means and he knows, with the terrible precision of a mind that never stops analyzing, that Persefoni just told him three things: (1) she was awake, (2) she heard everything, (3) she's angry not at the sex but at the carelessness — at being made an involuntary participant in something she didn't choose. His half-step backward is the Emissary retreating from a truth it can't narrate its way out of. The confusion on HIS face is different from Kathleen's — it's not "why is she angry?" but "how do I exist in this new reality where she knows?" His whole careful system of drawers and denials just got kicked open by a girl telling a story about a princess.

## Persefoni's Anger — What It Actually Is

She's angry because they were careless with her. Not because they had sex — she doesn't own them, she doesn't want Alejandro, she doesn't begrudge Kathleen the experience. She's angry because they did it six feet from her and didn't care enough to wonder if she was awake. She's angry because Kathleen — her Kathleen, her person, her seven-years-of-friendship person — crossed that gap without a thought about what it would mean for the person in the next bed. And she's angry because she had to lie still and protect THEM. She had to do the work of being invisible so they could have their moment, and nobody asked her to, and nobody will thank her for it, and the whole night she was the one holding still so that Kathleen could move.

There's also something underneath the anger that she won't name in this chapter: the grief from the yurt. The sense that the three of them just changed shape permanently and she didn't get a vote. The anger is real, but it's also a cover — it's easier to be furious about carelessness than to sit with the unnamed loss of something she can't describe.

## Scene F — After the Riff

The rest of the castle visit. Just the three of them — George and Rosemary are still at the pub across the road, which means there's no family buffer, no tourist-mode reset, no adult cheerfulness to absorb the tension. It's just the triangle, walking through ruins.

Kathleen is quiet. She's walking close to Persefoni but not talking, and her eyes keep flicking to Persefoni's face like she's reading weather. She's trying to figure out how bad this is. At some point she reaches for Persefoni's hand — a peace offering, an old habit, the way they've walked together since they were eight — and Persefoni takes it. She takes it because she loves Kathleen and because the anger is already losing to the love, and because she said what she needed to say and she's not the kind of person who holds a grudge. The hand-hold is forgiveness, or the beginning of it.

Alejandro walks slightly behind. He's looking at the castle but he's not seeing it. He's processing. His notebook is in his bag and he hasn't taken it out. He's got nothing to write. The classification system is still rebooting.

The chapter ends with Persefoni and Kathleen ahead on the path, hand in hand, and Persefoni pointing at something — a door, a window, a detail in the stone — and starting a new bit, a small one, something about the Norman soldiers being terrible at cooking, and Kathleen laughing, tentatively at first and then for real, and the sound of it carrying across the castle grounds. The wind through the stone. The laughter through the wind.
